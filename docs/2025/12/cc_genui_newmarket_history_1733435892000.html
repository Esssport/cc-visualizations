The History of Newmarket, Ontario

From Indigenous Heritage to Modern Urban Centre

Time Immemorial - 2025

Indigenous Heritage: Time Immemorial - 1787

The Original Inhabitants

For over 10,000 years, Indigenous peoples have inhabited what is now Ontario. The lands that would become Newmarket were home to the Wendat (Huron) and Anishinaabe (Ojibwe) peoples, who thrived with advanced social and economic systems, structures of law and democracy, and deeply rooted traditional knowledge.

Key Aspects of Indigenous Life

  • Hunting, fishing, and farming in rich forests and waterways
  • Key trade routes connecting Indigenous groups across Southern Ontario
  • Advanced social structures and governance systems
  • Millennia of sustainable land stewardship

Land Treaties and Displacement

1787 September 23: Negotiations began with the Mississaugas to purchase land along the north shore of Lake Ontario and the route from Lake Tarento (Toronto) to Lake Huron. The deal was obscured by a missing deed, creating uncertainty about legal tenure.

1818 October 17: The Treaty of 1818 completed the total surrender of territory from Lake Ontario to Georgian Bay, with four Chiefs of the Ojibway (Chippewa) nation participating. This was the most extensive treaty of all.

Starting in the 1820s, colonial administrators undertook many initiatives aimed at "civilizing" First Nations, including an early assimilation experiment at Coldwater-Narrows near Lake Simcoe.

The Founding Era: 1801-1820

1801: The Birth of Newmarket

1801 Settlement Founded
40 Farms Granted (200 acres each)
7 Sleighs Arrived (Rogers Family)

In the summer of 1801, Joseph Hill, a Quaker and perhaps Newmarket's first white citizen, dammed the East Holland River near Water and Main streets, creating the Millpond (known today as Fairy Lake). By the week before Christmas 1801, Hill had ground the first bushel of wheat produced locally at his grist-mill.

Timothy Rogers, a Loyalist from Vermont, arrived with his family in seven sleighs during winter 1801. He had received a grant for land totaling 40 farms, each of 200 acres.

Origin of the Name: The settlement was named "New Market" - in contrast to York (Toronto) as the "Old Market."

1802-1812: Quaker Community Growth

1802-1803: More Quakers arrived in the northern section of Yonge Street, along with tradesmen and entrepreneurs who filtered into the small settlement forming around Joseph Hill's mill on Water Street.

1803-1804: The settlement of Elisha Beman, a major local landowner and entrepreneur, provided a strong stimulus for community growth.

Religious Heritage

  • 1807: First Quaker meeting house built on Yonge Street
  • 1810-1812: Second Quaker meeting house constructed - the oldest religious building erected north of Toronto

The Age of Rebellion and Growth: 1820-1860

1837: The Mackenzie Rebellion

1837 August 3: Rebel leader William Lyon Mackenzie delivered a speech from the veranda of the North American Hotel in Newmarket. About 600 farmers and others sympathetic to Mackenzie's cause heard this speech, which contributed to the rebellion. Later that year, they armed themselves and marched down Yonge Street to take the capital.

This pivotal moment placed Newmarket at the heart of one of Canada's most significant historical events - the Upper Canada Rebellion of 1837.

1853: The Railway Arrives

1853 June: The first train pulled into Newmarket on the Toronto, Simcoe & Lake Huron Union Railroad - the first railway in Upper Canada. This transformative event opened new economic opportunities and connected Newmarket to Toronto's growing economy.

1857: Village Incorporation

Newmarket was incorporated as a village in 1857 with a population of 700.

Industrial Expansion: 1860-1920

1880: Town Status Achieved

2,000 Population
1880 Town Status

With a population of 2,000, Newmarket became a Town in 1880. William Cane was elected as its first mayor.

The list of reeves who served the Village of Newmarket pre-1881 included Donald Sutherland, Dr. James Hunter, Dr. Thomas Pyne, Alfred Boultbee, Samuel Roadhouse, Nelson Gorham, Erastus Jackson, and W.H. Ashworth.

Late 19th Century: Industrial Diversification

Newmarket transitioned from an agricultural hub to a diversified economy.

Major Industries

  • Davis Tannery: Major leather processing facility
  • Office Specialty Manufacturing: Business equipment production
  • Cane Furniture Factory: High-quality furniture manufacturing
  • Dixon Pencil Factory: Writing instrument production
  • Carriage-making
  • Brick manufacturing

Architectural Heritage

1883 The Old Town Hall, an Italianate building complete with a crested central bell-tower, was constructed. It remains a landmark today.

1902-1906: Electric Railway Era

The arrival of the electric railway in 1902 (later extended north to Jackson's Point and Sutton in 1906) facilitated growth and strengthened Newmarket's connection to Toronto's economy.

The Modern Era Begins: 1920-1980

Post-War Development Boom: 1950s

5,000 Population (1950)
11,000 Population (1970)

By the 1950s, the post-war development boom was well underway. New subdivisions were established and Newmarket experienced tremendous growth, with the town transforming from industrial center to primarily residential area.

Highway Era: 1980s Onwards

The expansion of Ontario Highway 400 to the west and the construction of Ontario Highway 404 to the east increasingly turned Newmarket into a bedroom community for Toronto.

Heritage Preservation

2013 October: Newmarket's downtown area, centered around Main Street, was recognized as a Provincial Heritage Conservation District. This status protects and officially recognizes the heritage sites and buildings along this historic thoroughfare.

Cultural Landmarks and Heritage

Fairy Lake

The heart of historic downtown Newmarket, Fairy Lake was created in 1801 when Joseph Hill built his mill and dammed the East Holland River.

Fairy Lake Features

  • 13.4 hectares of parkland, waterways, and marshland
  • Also known as Wesley Brooks Conservation Area
  • Picturesque waterfall at the foot of Main Street
  • Dam at Water Street bridge for flood control

Tom Taylor Trail

A recreation path following Fairy Lake and the East Holland River, featuring bridges, playgrounds, art installations, and gazebos. It's part of the larger Nokiidaa Trail stretching from Aurora to East Gwillimbury.

Museums and Cultural Centers

  • Elman W. Campbell Museum: Housed in the North York Registry Office, a Classical Revival-inspired building on Main Street
  • Old Town Hall (460 Botsford Street): Restored and renovated, offering community-based arts programs
  • Old Fire Hall (140 Main Street): Hosts arts-based programs and events
  • Riverwalk Commons: Features a plaque and mural noting the Roe & Borland Trading Post (1813)

Newmarket Today: Regional Seat of York

As the regional seat of York Region, Newmarket serves as a bedroom community to Toronto while maintaining its own distinct identity. The town continues to honor its Indigenous heritage through museum exhibits presenting the three Indigenous groups in Canada - First Nations, Inuit, and Metis - through Creation Stories, Grandfather Teachings, Medicine Wheel, and other traditions.

From its origins as a Quaker settlement on the banks of the Holland River to its status as a thriving urban growth centre in the Greater Toronto Area, Newmarket's journey reflects the broader story of Canadian growth, transformation, and the ongoing work of reconciliation with Indigenous peoples whose land this has been since time immemorial.
